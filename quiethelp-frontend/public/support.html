Your Rights & Indian Laws for Women Safety

India has several laws designed to protect women from harassment, violence, and discrimination. Here are the key laws you should know about.

Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act (2013)

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 protects women from harassment at their workplace.

  • Every organization must have an Internal Complaints Committee
  • Covers physical, verbal, or non-verbal harassment
  • Employer is responsible for providing safe environment
  • Complaint can be filed within 3 months of incident

Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (2005)

This act protects women from physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, and economic abuse by family members.

  • Protection against all forms of domestic abuse
  • Right to reside in shared household
  • Monetary relief and compensation
  • Protection orders and restraining orders available

IPC Section 354 - Outraging Modesty

Section 354 of Indian Penal Code deals with assault or use of criminal force on women with intent to outrage her modesty.

  • Imprisonment up to 5 years and/or fine
  • Covers physical contact and gestures
  • Includes stalking and voyeurism (354C, 354D)
  • Non-bailable offense in certain cases

IPC Section 509 - Insulting Modesty

Addresses any word, gesture, or act intended to insult the modesty of a woman.

  • Covers verbal and non-physical harassment
  • Includes obscene gestures and words
  • Imprisonment up to 3 years and/or fine
  • Applies to both public and private spaces

IT Act Section 67 - Cyber Harassment

Protection against cyber harassment, online stalking, and sharing of obscene content.

  • Covers electronic transmission of obscene material
  • Addresses online stalking and harassment
  • Includes revenge porn and morphed images
  • Imprisonment up to 5 years on first conviction

Dowry Prohibition Act (1961)

Makes giving and taking of dowry illegal and provides protection against dowry-related harassment.

  • IPC Section 498A covers dowry-related cruelty
  • Dowry death is punishable with life imprisonment

What to Do If You Face Harassment

Taking action can feel overwhelming, but you don't have to face this alone. Here are clear steps you can take.

1

Ensure Your Immediate Safety

Your safety is the top priority. If you're in immediate danger:

  • Move to a safe location - public place, friend's home, or police station
  • Call emergency services (100) or Women Helpline (1091) immediately
  • Keep your phone charged and with you
  • If possible, inform someone you trust about your situation
2

Document Everything

Evidence is crucial for any legal action. Document as much as possible:

  • Write down details: date, time, location, what happened
  • Save messages, emails, or any communication from the harasser
  • Take screenshots of online harassment
  • Note down any witnesses present
  • If there are physical injuries, take photographs
  • Keep medical reports if you seek medical attention
3

Report to Authorities

You have the right to file a complaint. Choose based on your situation:

  • Police Station: File an FIR (First Information Report) - this is your legal right
  • Women's Cell: Most police stations have a dedicated women's cell
  • Online Complaint: Use cybercrime.gov.in for cyber harassment
  • Workplace: Report to Internal Complaints Committee if harassment is at work
  • National Commission for Women: Call 7827-170-170 or visit ncw.nic.in
4

Seek Support

Don't go through this alone. Reach out for emotional and practical support:

  • Talk to someone you trust - family member, friend, or counselor
  • Contact support helplines for guidance and counseling
  • Join support groups to connect with others who've had similar experiences
  • Consider professional counseling for emotional healing
  • Legal aid services can help if you need legal representation
5

Know Your Legal Options

Understanding your legal rights empowers you to take action:

  • File an FIR - police cannot refuse to register your complaint
  • Request a female officer if you feel more comfortable
  • You can file a complaint at any police station (not just local jurisdiction)
  • Seek a protection order or restraining order through court
  • Consult a lawyer - many offer free legal aid to women
  • Follow up on your case regularly

Safety Tips & Precautions

Prevention and awareness can help keep you safer. Here are practical tips for different situations.

üè†

At Home

  • Keep emergency numbers saved and easily accessible
  • Have a trusted neighbor or friend you can contact quickly
  • Keep your phone charged and with you
  • Know the location of nearest police station
  • Install safety apps on your phone
  • Don't share your daily routine on social media
üöó

While Traveling

  • Share your travel details with someone you trust
  • Keep GPS/location services on
  • Sit near the driver in public transport when possible
  • Avoid isolated areas, especially at night
  • Book verified cabs and share trip details
  • Trust your instincts - if something feels wrong, act on it
üíº

At Workplace

  • Know about your company's anti-harassment policy
  • Document any inappropriate behavior immediately
  • Report to Internal Complaints Committee
  • Keep communication professional and documented
  • Avoid being alone with someone who makes you uncomfortable
  • Build a support network among colleagues
üì±

Online Safety

  • Use strong, unique passwords for all accounts
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Be careful about sharing personal information
  • Block and report harassers immediately
  • Review privacy settings on social media
  • Save evidence of online harassment (screenshots)
üåô

When Out at Night

  • Stay in well-lit, populated areas
  • Travel in groups when possible
  • Keep someone informed of your location
  • Avoid shortcuts through isolated areas
  • Keep your phone accessible and charged
  • Be aware of your surroundings at all times
üë•

In Social Situations

  • Don't leave your drink unattended
  • Have a buddy system with friends
  • Set up code words with friends for emergencies
  • Trust your gut feeling about people
  • Don't feel pressured to stay in uncomfortable situations
  • It's okay to say no and leave

How to File an Official Complaint Online

Follow these steps to register your complaint on government portals

1

Choose the Right Portal

Select the appropriate portal based on your complaint type:

  • Cybercrime Portal: For online harassment, stalking, morphing, or any cyber offense
  • NCW Portal: For women's rights violations, general harassment, discrimination
  • SHe-Box: Specifically for workplace sexual harassment
  • Local Police: For physical harassment, assault, or immediate danger
2

Register on the Portal

Most portals require registration before filing a complaint:

  • Provide basic details: name, contact number, email address
  • Create a secure password for your account
  • Verify your mobile number or email through OTP
  • Note: Your identity is protected and not shared with the accused
3

Fill Complaint Details

Provide clear and detailed information about the incident:

  • Date and Time: When did the incident occur?
  • Location: Where did it happen? (online/offline address)
  • Details: What exactly happened? Be specific but concise
  • Accused Details: Name, contact, or any identifying information (if known)
  • Witnesses: Were there any witnesses? Provide their details if possible
4

Upload Evidence

Strong evidence strengthens your case. Upload relevant documents:

  • Screenshots of messages, emails, or social media posts
  • Photos or videos (if applicable)
  • Audio recordings (where legal)
  • Medical reports (in case of physical harm)
  • Any other supporting documents
  • Ensure files are in accepted formats (usually PDF, JPEG, PNG)
5

Submit & Track Your Complaint

After submitting, keep track of your complaint status:

  • Note your complaint reference number - you'll need this to track progress
  • You'll receive a confirmation on your registered email/mobile
  • Login to the portal to check status updates regularly
  • Authorities may contact you for additional information
  • If no action is taken within reasonable time, follow up or escalate
  • Keep all communication records for your reference
